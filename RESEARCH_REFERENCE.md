## KIKOPUP (EMILY LARLHAM) METHODOLOGY â€” Key Principles

**Source:** Dogmantics.com, progressivereinforcementtraining.com, Kikopup YouTube channel

### Progressive Reinforcement Training (PRT) Core Rules:
1. **No punishment, intimidation, or physical corrections** â€” ever
2. **Reward desired behaviors** with food, play, praise, or life rewards
3. **Tiny progressive steps** â€” break everything into the smallest possible increments
4. **Capture natural behaviors** when possible (e.g., capturing calmness)
5. **Criterion: 80% success over 3 sessions** before advancing â€” from applied behavior analysis standard
6. **Regression is normal** â€” going back a step is always good practice, never failure
7. **Short sessions** â€” 3-5 minutes optimal for retention. Multiple short sessions > one long session
8. **Train multiple different behaviors per day** for best engagement and retention
9. **The dog always has a choice** â€” if they move away, let them. Never force or restrain.
10. **Never use the dog's name negatively** â€” it should always predict good things

### Key Kikopup Concepts Referenced in App:

**It's Yer Choice (Impulse Control):** Hold treat in closed fist. Dog learns that backing off/looking away = reward. Never take the forbidden item as reward â€” always reward from OTHER hand.

**Capturing Calmness:** Reward the dog for naturally offered calm states. No cue, no prompting. You're reinforcing a state of being. Quiet marker, gentle treat delivery. Over time, do it on a mat to build mat = calm association.

**Positive Interrupter:** A special emergency sound (kissy noise, whistle) that means "stop everything and come get something AMAZING." Must be charged with 15-20+ pairings before testing. Must ALWAYS predict jackpot rewards. Never overuse or it loses power. Never use when dog is over threshold.

**Leave It vs Drop It:** Explicitly separated as different skills:
- **Leave It** = "don't touch it in the first place" (prevention)
- **Drop It** = "release what's in your mouth" (trade game â€” always trade UP, always give the toy BACK)

**Leash Walking (CONNECTED methodology):** Must be reliable INDOORS before outdoor proofing. Indoor LLW â†’ Outdoor LLW is a hard prerequisite chain.

**Separation Training:** 
- Emily Larlham's advice from Dogmantics: "For dogs anxious about being alone, ignore him upon entering and leaving your house. Save hellos for later â€” e.g., half an hour after returning. This prevents creating a huge contrast between alone time and your arrival."
- Graduated departures: 1 second â†’ 5 â†’ 10 â†’ 30 seconds â†’ 1 min â†’ 2 min â†’ 5 â†’ 15 min (hardest barrier) â†’ 30 â†’ 60+
- Fake departure cues: Put on shoes/coat but don't leave. Pick up keys, sit down. Desensitize the cues.
- Film the dog to watch for stress signals during absence
- First 15 minutes is the hardest part â€” most anxious responses occur within first 40 minutes
- Leave enrichment (Kong, puzzle toy) but note: truly anxious dogs won't eat when stressed

**Cooperative Care / Handling:** Based on Chirag Patel's Bucket Game + Kikopup protocols. Touch = treat, brief touch first, build duration. Always at dog's pace. If she moves away, PAUSE â€” never hold still or restrain.

### Skill Progression Pattern (consistent across all Kikopup skills):
1. **Capture or Lure** the behavior (no cue yet)
2. **Build reliability** (dog offers behavior consistently)
3. **Add verbal cue** BEFORE the behavior (word â†’ behavior â†’ mark â†’ treat)
4. **Fade the lure** (smaller hand motion â†’ empty hand â†’ just the word)
5. **Add duration** (hold position longer before marking)
6. **Add distance** (further from you)
7. **Add distractions** (start easy, increase gradually)
8. **Proof in new environments** (lower criteria in harder contexts, rebuild)

## DOG ANXIETY SIGNALS â€” Body Language Reference

**Source:** AKC, VCA Animal Hospitals, Turid Rugaas (calming signals), Dogs Trust

### Calming Signals (Turid Rugaas identified 30+ signals):
Dogs use these to communicate discomfort AND to self-soothe. Context is key â€” these are only stress signals when they occur in inappropriate contexts.

| Signal | Description | Stress indicator when... |
|---|---|---|
| **Lip licking** | Quick tongue flick over lips | Not eating/drinking, in new situation |
| **Yawning** | Exaggerated, wide yawn | Not just waking up, tense face |
| **Panting** | Open-mouth breathing | Not hot or exercised, stiff drawn-back tongue |
| **Pacing** | Walking back and forth | Can't settle, no apparent purpose |
| **Trembling/shaking** | Full body shaking | Not wet, not cold, "shake off" after stress |
| **Whale eye** | Showing whites of eyes | Head turned but eyes watching fearfully |
| **Tucked tail** | Tail held low/between legs | In social or new situations |
| **Raised hackles** | Hair along spine stands up | Meeting new dogs, sudden stimuli |
| **Drooling** | Excessive salivation | Not near food, breeds that don't normally drool |
| **Scratching** | Sudden intense scratching | No apparent itch, in stressful context |
| **Hiding** | Going under furniture, behind you | When visitors arrive, loud noises |
| **Clingy/velcro** | Following room to room constantly | Pre-departure, new environment |
| **Freezing** | Sudden complete stillness | Mid-action, assessing danger |
| **Ground sniffing** | Sudden interest in ground | When approached by stranger/dog |
| **Turning away/head turn** | Deliberately looking away | When something approaches |
| **Low body posture** | Crouching, trying to appear small | New environments, loud noises |
| **Appeasement grin** | Showing teeth without aggression | Trying to defuse social conflict |
| **Ears pinned back** | Ears flattened against head | Fearful or submissive |
| **Avoidance** | Approach-retreat pattern | Wanting to investigate but afraid |
| **Reduced appetite** | Refusing treats/food | Normally food-motivated dog refuses |

### Separation Anxiety Specific Signs:
- Vocalization within minutes of departure (barking, howling, whining)
- Destruction focused on exit points (doors, windows) or owner possessions
- Elimination despite being house-trained
- Excessive greeting behavior on return
- Pre-departure anxiety (reacting to departure cues: keys, shoes, coat)
- Refusal to eat when alone
- Following owner room to room (velcro dog)
- Most anxious behavior occurs within first 20-40 minutes

### Departure Cues to Desensitize:
- Picking up keys
- Putting on shoes/boots
- Putting on coat
- Picking up bag/purse
- Opening/closing doors
- Starting car
- Saying specific goodbye phrases
- Specific time-of-day routines (morning work routine)
